Settings redesign

Product

Kontur.Market

Year

2023

I redesigned Market’s settings page, improving the user experience and refreshing the appearance. This work helped to increase customer satisfaction and strengthen the code infrastructure. The solution also became a foundation for the follow-up release to boost upselling inside the app.

My contribution included justifying the task to business, doing user research, designing the interfaces, supervising the development, testing the implementation, and collecting user feedback.

About the product

Kontur.Market is a web application for small and grown-up businesses. It helps business owners to automate accounting and document exchange with controlling authorities. It also provides tools for performance analytics and decision-making.

The app has over 45,000 monthly active users.

Backstory

Like many web apps, Market has a settings section. There, users can review their personal data and billing information, manage third-party service integrations, and set other preferences.

A few years ago, Market was an emerging service with limited functionality, so there wasn’t much to configure — all the controls fit inside a small side panel. As the product grew, the scope of supported features and connections widened substantially. The rise brought along the need for dozens of new settings, which felt too much for the modest side panel UI. The overload of content made it hard to scan and navigate through:

All visuals on this page were originally in Russian. I translated them into English to provide more context for the reader.

Our team, especially the designers, were dissatisfied with how confusing and cluttered Settings became. No one ever got around to fixing the problem, though — we thought the settings section was purely technical and rarely visited by users. Updating the UI was expected to require more resources than any measurable profit would make up for.

I decided to investigate the case and find the point of interest intersection for the users, the business, and the design.

Reaching out to business

The controls in the settings panel supported many different parts of Market. My idea was to approach product managers across all these departments and ask them to open up a little about related plans. After that, we brainstormed whether we could move towards fulfilling these ambitions by redesigning Settings.

Among all the conversations with the decision-makers, one got things moving. The owner of our billing department shared that they were looking for a way to boost KPIs in upselling. The studies on the subject showed that users rarely purchased add-ons from the Market app. One of the reasons was that the app only offered additional modules contextually, for example, when users attempted to access a feature only available on a higher-tier plan. To explore all tariffs and options, the customers had to visit the dedicated landing page — which wasn’t easily discoverable from the app itself.

Talking about possible solutions, the owner mentioned the concept of a page inside Market, where users could see personalised deals according to their already-purchased modules and business field. At that time, he had been thinking about it for quite a while but could not shape a vision of any viable implementation. To me, that sounded like a match. I laid out my concerns about the outdated settings section and supposed that renewing it might as well provide a necessary base for “the Showcase page”.

We found the notion hopeful but needed some artefacts to proceed. I took time to materialise the concept and come back to have it evaluated by the team.

User research

After the talks with the managers, I got a good picture of the new Settings. But before making any sketches, I decided to get some user feedback to ensure the design would align with customers’ needs. Together with our UX research team, we did a two-step study to understand users’ thoughts on the current Settings, reveal usability issues, and collect ideas for improvement.

The first part of the research was semi-structured interviews, which confirmed our team’s assumptions. The participants described existing Settings as disorganised and overly complex. They also complained about the ambiguous, sometimes insufficient status indication. One of the respondents said: “Not too much is clear here, so I don’t see this [page] often. If I needed to tweak anything, I’d more likely contact the support”. These insights highlighted that functionality and simplicity should be a top concern during the section redesign.

Secondly, we set up a series of card-sorting sessions. The goal was to understand how our users classify settings in Market and how they motivate the grouping. In each session, we asked the participants to put configurable entities into the predefined categories. To reduce bias, we did the testing using several slightly different category lists. Here are some of the complete mappings:

The results weren’t straightforward. The users clustered most of the entities similarly, which suggested a way to organise the corresponding settings in the new UI. At the same time, there were some contrasting choices the participants made. As the follow-up conversations showed, the approach to the task depended on the type of each customer’s business and their working routine. I concluded that developing a separate Settings structure for each user persona would be impractical. Instead, I focused on building a universal, expressive arrangement that would be easy to learn and use for all.

Concept

At the time, our design team was doing a big cleanup across the whole application, refreshing visual style and unifying interface patterns. Drafting the global layout of the new Settings, I wanted to get some inspiration from the already-compiled and time-tested layouts.

I liked the format of Market’s Reports page, where different data types show up in separate blocks called “widgets.” The widgets display key metrics for quick assessments, and clicking a block opens a detailed report:

The segmented nature of Reports appealed to me: it provided a good overview of the diverse page content yet didn’t overwhelm with too much info upfront. This modular UI allowed for more precise and effective user onboarding than standard linear layouts.

Concept Validation

For the validation, I prepared an interactive prototype. In the prototype, one could open the new Settings from the Market's Dashboard screen, jump between grouped preferences via the sidebar, and change some of them:

First, during moderated tests, I evaluated the concept with some of our most loyal customers. They received the design well. However, some users remarked on the unfamiliar naming in the sidebar, which made them “click through every subsection to find the new locations of known settings”.

I shared this point with fellow designers as I passed them the prototype. We agreed that the issue wasn’t critical: regardless of the labelling, users would have to explore the interface the first time they see it — and we expected to steepen the learning curve with an onboarding tour. The discussion with the designers also drew my attention to several essential details to polish in the final solution.

Next, I presented the concept to our developers and asked them if rebuilding Settings could benefit the code infrastructure. After a short code review, the devs pointed out that the task itself would be resource-heavy, but the positive impact would become more and more prominent over time. With both frontend and backend code refactored, Settings and the connected app modules would be much easier to support and evolve.

Finally, I came back to the billing department owner. In addition to the main interactive prototype, I demonstrated its modified version where “the Showcase page” appeared as a default subsection:

I backed up that demo with the gathered motivation, which ultimately sold the idea to the stakeholder — he was ready to allocate his resource quota for the development. Planning the further work, we settled that the functionality would be shipped to users gradually, and the upselling page would only be released after the Settings layout updates.

Documentation

At first glance, Improving Settings seemed like purely cosmetic work. However, the concept phase made it clear that for a quality implementation, we had to account for many subtleties, dependencies, and user scenarios. That’s why thorough analytic documentation was a must.

Together with our analysts and project managers, we recorded all possible states of then-current settings and related business logic. Then, we tidied it up and set down the “to-be” requirements. In addition, we collected interaction metrics on the existing “side-panel” Settings.

I used this data to ensure the final design was thoughtful and inclusive.

Design

In its ultimate form, the new interface covered a lot of different aspects of the task and had several specific mechanics involved. I describe the most notable ones below.

Global layout

Since the concept, a few things changed in the global layout. Most of the refinements were inspired by the ideas put forward by analysts and designers during concept validation.

Relying on the documentation and user research, I regrouped the widgets and optimised the sidebar by making subsection titles more concise and removing nesting. I also took away the redundant titles on top of a selected section. As a result, the UI became lighter and better for intuitive user navigation.

Widget components

Thanks to the exhaustive documentation produced earlier, I created a kit of block-type UI components that one could use to quickly build any setting widget or modify an existing one. Every such component is a wrapper over elements from our team’s React library, which means the specialised kit is easy to replicate and maintain in code.

Selective editing

At the time, our design team was switching to selective editing all across the Market UI. In this mechanic, a page appears in read-only mode by default, while users can independently put its parts into edit mode.

In the early prototypes, whole widgets could toggle between view and edit states, and all changes could be saved with a single button click. This approach got a fair share of criticism from my fellow designers. The main problem was with the complex parameters that were not just edited as plain text but had a dedicated interface instead. Embedding this UI into widgets seemed tedious and could lead to content abruptly jumping when the user goes from view to edit mode and back.

There were other minor issues with the above idea. For example, it was unclear how binary settings should behave, as the nature of a toggle control entails that the changes take effect immediately and do not require confirmation. One more potential drawback had to do with displaying uneditable fields — those would have to look “disabled” in the edit mode. Implementing this would only waste developers' time and make the layout messier.

I reconsidered the editing and made it more granular. Now, users can interact with each setting independently by clicking on it. Basic settings can be changed on the spot, while more elaborate ones open modal windows. If multiple actions are available for a setting, they move into a dropdown menu:

I like the way it works. With selective editing, Settings better aid user focus, helping minimise errors and improve performance in related scenarios.

State indication

One of the main flaws of the old Settings was their low informativity. The statuses of connections and other configurations lacked clarity and, in some cases, were not displayed at all. Because of this, users had to spend extra time figuring out whether they had set up a particular feature correctly or if any action was required.

In the redesign, I added success badges. They appear in widgets of active integrations, allowing users to quickly see what functionality is enabled. The on-hover tooltips explicitly convey the meaning of the badges:

Of course, there can also be warnings and errors. These showed up inconsistently in the previous interface: it could be a vibrantly-coloured text, a single icon, both or none. Now, all such states are communicated concisely through compact notification blocks — the same ones we use widely across Market. If there’s at least one notification in a Settings subsection, the marker appears in the sidebar, and the Settings button in the top bar fills with colour. This hint guarantees that the user won’t miss the alert regardless of what page they are currently on. Redirecting to Settings

Settings are not always opened directly from the Market top bar. Sometimes, users get there from other app sections or through the quick search field on the Home screen. In these scenarios, the old UI was confusing: the side panel just slid into view, exposing all available configurations. Users had to go through this long list to find the setting they needed, which held back the workflow.

I fixed this in the new solution. Now, when visited from any other in-app page, Settings open at the right subsection. Then, the target widget flashes to draw the user’s attention to the relevant content.

Development & testing

I included all the principles above and the actual designs into a complete developer handoff. Additionally, I hosted a presentation where I showed the solution to the team, pointing out the most exciting aspects and answering questions.

The development spanned two work sprints and took almost half a year. During this time, I was thoroughly curating the process. Every day, we had short meetings to update each other on the ongoing and completed tasks and tackle immediate problems. Sometimes, we made agreements that demanded changes in the documentation or the prototypes — I made sure to revise the artefacts in time.

Over those six months, several other tasks went through development that touched Settings interface-wise or shared their back end. That is why the most significant challenge for our feature team was to debate and agree on decisions regarding release sequencing, resolving intertask conflicts and dealing with code dependencies.

During testing, I joined our QA engineers to review the in-product implementation of Settings. I did a few iterations of inspection focusing on appearance and UX mechanics. After every iteration, I passed the bugs to the testers and developers for fixes. That ultimately made the new section look and behave just like in the design.

Impact

By refreshing Settings, we levelled up one of the most essential interfaces in the Market. The new UI is more organised, user-friendly, sustainable, and visually pleasing.

The task didn’t aim for any short-term money profit, so getting users' feedback was crucial to check if we were moving in the right direction. I introduced a small banner at the top of the released section. Users saw it after they spent some time working with settings. One could rate the update on a scale of 1 to 5 and optionally write a review:

Over the first month after the rollout, I collected over 150 responses. They showed that the redesign was welcomed well — 84% of the participants awarded it four or five stars:

Just like after any major product update, there was negative feedback, too. Unfortunately, each of the low ratings came with no review attached. Together with the researchers, I plan to contact the unsatisfied users to ask them about any specific complaints and suggestions they might have. If the concerns are rational, I will address them as I improve Settings further.

Support & scaling

Upgrading the old Settings was beneficial for the app infrastructure. We refactored dated code and reinforced the front end with the component-based widget structure. Making routine fixes and new functionality is now less costly for both designers and developers.

With the new modular structure and user experience patterns involved, Settings are now on their way to becoming more than just a utility screen. In further releases, the Billing widget would upscale into a customer-tailored page that would encourage upselling of Market tariffs and add-ons and increase the revenue.

I brought the concepts of this ‘showcasing subsection’ to the finished look with the help of a fellow designer, Daniil Imgada. We improved the layout and made the page more engaging by adding illustrations drawn by Daniil and our graphic designer, Polina Molchanova.

As the feature lead, I continue to direct how Settings evolve. I indend is to keep the global layout clear and consistent — both graphically and in terms of interactions. Since the redesigned section can now serve marketing needs, it would be vital to set the limits. The users have to be able to complete setting scenarios effectively and not be frustrated by the ads. There has to be a middle ground between the product and the business.
