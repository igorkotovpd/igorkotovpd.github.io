Responsive UI

Product

Kontur.Kassa

Year

2021

I improved Kassa’s accessibility by making its layouts responsive. I developed scenario-motivated resizing rules and designed interchangeable variations of the UI components. These enhancements brought in new users, made the product more robust and attractive, and opened new expansion opportunities.

My contribution included metrics analysis, proving the motivation to stakeholders, interface design, developer handoff and consultation, hands-on implementation testing, and working with the post-release user feedback.

About the product

Kontur.Kassa is a POS application for retail and catering businesses. It helps cashiers and servers to take orders, process payments, and register necessary receipts. Kassa receives inventory data from Kontur.Market and sends back transaction history used for business analytics.

The app has over 40,000 monthly active users on Windows and Android.

Backstory

The development of Kassa for Windows began in 2017. Working on the MVP, the team had to compromise on resource limitations and strict deadlines, so they cut out or overlooked some parts of the implementation.

One of those aspects was UI adaptivity. Back then, we presumed the app to be run only on standard-sized displays and specific preconfigured devices, so we only targeted the screen ratios of 16:10 and 16:9. For quite a long time, it had been just fine. The team never received user complaints or had other reasons to move the responsive features up in the task queue.

A few years after the first release of Kassa, our marketing team ran a promotion campaign aimed at attracting new audience from competitors’ solutions. They found out that the users willing to switch were more likely to do so, provided they could keep the hardware they were already working on. That hardware included displays — which were different from the ones we supported. It became clear that poor UI scalability was no longer just a backlog record but a restraining factor for business growth.

It was a perfect moment to take the lead and look into the problem.

Inspection

First, I decided to study the situation to determine how critical the expected issues were. I decreased the width of the application window and tried to complete the most basic user scenarios. This test session revealed many bugs of different severity: some were only visual defects, while others badly affected user experience or made whole scenarios incompletable:

All visuals on this page were originally in Russian. I translated them into English to provide more context for the reader.

The abundance of resizing issues inspired me to widen the scope of the desired fix. I wanted the solution to eliminate the errors locally and introduce universal in-app adaptivity guides. I believed that adhering to these rules in design and development would boost the coherence and robustness of Kassa’s UI.

Proving value

I assumed that the task would be costly in development. That’s why, before committing to it further, I decided to get solid proof that the scaling issues were worth fixing. For that, I wanted to get the upper bound of users who would benefit from the solution, counting the potential app adopters interviewed during the promo campaign and our then-present users. The former was easy to obtain — I got the necessary data from our marketing team. The latter was a bit more tricky.

Firstly, I reached out to our customer support to see if there were any inquiries related to corrupt UI. Surprisingly, we found very few of them. That could have stopped the research, but I had to dig deeper. With the help of our analysts and developers, I collected the effective resolutions of displays that our users worked on:

After doing some math on these data and clustering the dimensions by screen ratio, I calculated the share of the existing Kassa users likely affected by UI adaptivity bugs.

Gluing together all the gathered info gave me quite a figure. Making the app responsive would improve the usability experience for about 24% of our Windows users. This strong point helped me pitch the idea to the stakeholders — I got the green light to move on and elaborate on the solution.

Design

During the design phase, I focused on layouts covering all the primary in-app scenarios and most of the rarer ones.

Menu screens

Kassa’s menu is like a cashier’s back office. Its starting point is the Shift summary screen, a dashboard with key sales insights and easy access to the often-used features.

The main adaptivity problem here was with the quick actions panel. Its fixed width made the actions overlap with the shift statistics on narrow screens, making the summary illegible:

I aimed to keep the shift stats readable regardless of the screen width. I also wanted the quick actions to always be available without scrolling for fast user workflows.

As a solution, I suggested displaying action shortcuts differently depending on the window width. If there’s enough space, the items show up the regular way — in a block. Otherwise, the actions appear as buttons. To save precious vertical room, the buttons only take up one row. If some actions can’t fit in, they hide inside the “More” dropdown. The order in which the actions are hidden correlates with how popular each one is — I obtained this data from our analysts. Here’s what it looks like:

Under the other menu sections, there’s more detailed info about receipts, sold items, and cash operations. There are many tables and lists, so the issues were mostly wrong formatting and text truncation.

The fixes were straightforward. I depicted the desired behaviour in the auto-layout prototypes. Here are the “before“ and the “after“:

As seen from the two demos above, I also worked on the global layout. I defined the percentage rule for how the menu and the content share the screen and specified the containers’ min-width values. With these tweaks, the window area is used more effectively, and the UI looks balanced on both wide and compact monitors.

Fill-up screen

The Fill-up screen is the most critical piece of UI in Kassa — every sale starts here. Cashiers can add items to the cart and modify and delete entities. The added items are on the left, while the right side has the search bar and the tiles for quickly adding popular goods.

I identified two major flaws in this interface. The first one was the broken resizing behaviour of the cart panel. Strangely enough, the width of this layout depended on the window height. That caused the item list to either collapse too much or appear overly large on non-standard displays. Furthermore, the total amount on the primary "To payment" button could get pushed off the frame and become invisible to the user:

To make things right, I set the cart panel’s x-size to rely on the screen width instead of its height. I also adjusted the cart container’s minimum width and improved the primary button to dynamically change the content based on free horizontal space. These tweaks ensure the user can read the cart total no matter what:

Another problem was the inflexible item tiles. Changing the screen width made the grid scale as a whole, where each tile could grow or shrink infinitely. In these conditions, tiles became practically useless on wide monitors: their titles were harder to scan, and smaller hitboxes took more effort to aim at:

The tile grid is a susceptible area UX-wise. Our customers usually configure the tiles right after they buy Kassa and set up all the hardware. The cashiers get used to the tile arrangement over time, so any unexpected changes in the layout can bring discomfort and temporarily slow down UI interaction. That is why my concern was not only to adapt the tiles to various screen sizes but also to minimise the troubling impact on current users.

I assigned each tile min-width and max-width values, which I determined empirically based on our users’ display dimensions. The length of a tile row stopped being static: Kassa would now try to fit in as many as it could within the tile size constraints:

Payment screen

Every sale ends on the Payment screen. Here, cashiers take payments, apply discounts, and print receipts. The interface is dense: a right-side menu, a panel in the centre, and a list of items on the left. The hardcoded widths of the first two made the cart layout squeeze and break as the app window got thinner:

Both the payment menu and the panel hold quite a bit of information, which is contextually essential for the user. It wasn’t possible to hide any of it without drastic changes to the existing design, and rebuilding the layout felt beyond the scope of dealing with responsiveness.

I thought a more manageable way might be not to show the cart on compact screens. I assumed that doing so would not hurt user experience since the contents of a sale aren’t that relevant at the payment stage. To make it certain, I asked our UX researchers for help. We conducted a series of interviews, where most participants stated they “liked” the item list on the Payment screen. However, no one could name a viable reason they still needed the cart after fill-up. We agreed that my hypothesis was confirmed — even considering possible bias.

In the adaptivity rules, I mentioned the breakpoint value — if the screen width goes below it, the item panel disappears to provide extra space for the most crucial UI. I also capped the size of the central panel so that the numpad stays ergonomic:

Development & testing

In this case, the development and the testing intertwined with the design and were carried out in parallel. Over the first weeks of prototyping, our front-end developers put up the UI mockups on Storybook. As the work progressed, I iteratively reviewed how the interface behaved on different screen widths, proposed suggestions and corrected the guides.

When we settled all the adaptivity rules, I thoroughly described them in the designs. After we shipped the new logic to our users’ devices, I hosted a presentation for the team to get us all in sync. I spoke about the improvements, reflected on the process and the outcome, and put forward future possibilities.

Impact

Making Kassa’s UI adaptive let our team earn new customers who have switched to us from competing software. Within the first six months after the updates came out, 41% of the campaign-discovered potential adopters became our users. Overall, we levelled up the interface accessibility for 19% of our audience on Windows.

The product became a more appealing solution for businesses that have yet to work with Kassa. Implementing responsiveness helped us keep up with the leading apps in the field.

We encountered some negative feedback from the continuing users, though — there were inquiries on the shortened rows of item tiles. We expected these issues, but the reason for the complaints surprised us. Most complaining customers had their tiles set up to fit into the viewport. The applied adaptivity constraints made the tile grid grow vertically and brought up the need for the scroll. As we found out later, scrolling delays the workflow, so users try to avoid it at any cost. Luckily, tiles in Kassa can be easily rearranged and organised into groups. We got the users through the process so that their tiles returned to being just a tap away. The problem was resolved.

This situation exposed a remarkable UX insight and highlighted the importance of determining the genuine reasons for users’ reactions and distinguishing between emotional and rational feedback.

Support & scaling

Giving our interfaces stronger scalability resulted in refactoring the core app code, making it easier to read and maintain. But more notably — we set a higher standard for future development. All designs are now prepared in several differently-sized layouts and equipped with adaptivity guides. If possible, we try to reuse resizing patterns across similar UI elements. Finally, we do hands-on testing to monitor the quality of the implementation.

I believe that all the work on the scalable UI pays off in the long run. It boosts the product’s overall quality, making it more accessible and loved by customers. Conforming to adaptivity standards would allow Kassa to expand across a broader range of devices. Ultimately, it means better sales and higher user satisfaction.
